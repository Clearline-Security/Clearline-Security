# The SCOPE Framework

## A Threat Modeling Framework for High-Risk Individuals, Journalists, and Human Rights Defenders

**Developed by Clearline Security**

---

## Abstract

Traditional threat modeling methodologies were developed primarily for software systems, enterprise networks, and cloud infrastructure. While these methodologies effectively identify technical vulnerabilities, they often fail to address the broader operational realities faced by journalists, activists, whistleblowers, researchers, and other individuals operating in hostile environments.

The SCOPE Framework (Security-Centered Operational Protection & Exposure) is a people-centric threat modeling methodology designed to evaluate and mitigate risks across an individual's entire operational ecosystem. Rather than focusing exclusively on digital assets, SCOPE recognizes that people, communications, devices, documents, identities, and daily operational practices form an interconnected security environment. Compromise of any single element may expose the entire network.

The framework emphasizes systematic identification of protected assets, adversary capabilities, attack vectors, operational consequences, and layered defensive measures. It is intended for use before sensitive investigations, travel, campaigns, or other high-risk activities — and as a recurring process throughout an operation.

---

## 1. Introduction

Modern threats extend far beyond malware and software exploits. Investigative journalists, activists, civil society organizations, and human rights defenders frequently face coordinated campaigns that combine cyberattacks, social engineering, physical surveillance, legal pressure, and information operations.

In many cases, the objective is not merely to compromise a device — but to identify confidential sources, map professional relationships, disrupt investigations, intimidate individuals, or undermine trust.

Traditional security models frequently prioritize the protection of computers and networks. SCOPE instead prioritizes the protection of people and the relationships that enable their work.

The central principle of the framework is:

> **Protect the operational ecosystem, not merely the individual.**

---

## 2. Core Philosophy

Every interaction generates operational risk.

A journalist's phone contains contacts.  
Those contacts reveal sources.  
Sources reveal investigations.  
Investigations reveal future activities.  
Future activities reveal opportunities for surveillance or disruption.

Security therefore cannot be limited to technology alone. Every person, communication channel, physical location, and operational routine becomes part of the threat model.

SCOPE treats the entire operational ecosystem as a collection of interconnected assets whose protection requires continuous assessment.

---

## 3. Framework Overview

The SCOPE Framework consists of six analytical phases:

1. Identify who is being protected
2. Identify what requires protection
3. Identify the adversary
4. Identify potential attack vectors
5. Evaluate operational consequences
6. Implement layered operational security controls

Each phase informs the next, creating a repeatable threat modeling process.

---

## 4. Phase One — Identify Who Is Being Protected

Threat modeling begins by defining the subject of protection.

Typical subjects include:

- Investigative journalists
- Human rights defenders
- Political activists
- Non-governmental organizations
- Researchers
- Whistleblowers
- Independent media organizations

Understanding the subject's role establishes the context for the remainder of the assessment. The same adversary may pose different risks depending on the subject's activities, visibility, and operational environment.

---

## 5. Phase Two — Identify What Is Being Protected

Unlike traditional frameworks that emphasize information systems, SCOPE considers every element of the operational ecosystem to be an asset.

### People

People represent the highest-value assets.

Examples include: confidential sources, professional contacts, editors, legal counsel, family members, colleagues.

Exposure of a confidential source may create irreversible harm. Accordingly, human relationships receive the highest protection priority.

### Communications

Communication channels include: encrypted messaging, email, telephone calls, video conferences, contact lists, communication metadata.

Metadata alone may reveal relationships even when message contents remain encrypted.

### Devices

Devices often serve as gateways into the broader operational ecosystem: smartphones, laptops, tablets, cameras, external storage, hardware security keys.

### Information

Information assets include: research notes, draft articles, photographs, audio recordings, video evidence, credentials, encryption keys, internal documentation.

### Identity

Operational identities include: legal identity, professional identity, anonymous identities, pseudonymous accounts, online presence, professional reputation.

### Physical Assets

Physical environments frequently expose operational information: home, office, vehicles, hotels, meeting locations, travel routes.

### Digital Infrastructure

Infrastructure supporting operations includes: cloud storage, websites, domains, backup systems, collaboration platforms.

### Operational Information

Operational data includes: investigation timelines, travel plans, funding information, meeting schedules, organizational structure, future projects.

---

## 6. Phase Three — Identify the Adversary

Risk depends largely on adversary capability. SCOPE categorizes adversaries according to resources, authority, and operational sophistication.

| Tier | Adversary | Capability |
|------|-----------|------------|
| A1 | Opportunistic attacker | Low |
| A2 | Online harasser / targeted individual | Moderate |
| A3 | Organized crime | High |
| A4 | Private intelligence firms | High |
| A5 | Government agencies | Very High |
| A6 | Intelligence services / nation-state | Exceptional |

Each adversary should be evaluated according to:

- Motivation
- Technical capability
- Financial resources
- Legal authority
- Physical access
- Surveillance capability
- Insider recruitment capability

**Understanding capability is often more important than understanding intent.**

---

## 7. Phase Four — Identify Attack Vectors

SCOPE recognizes that sophisticated campaigns rarely rely on a single technique.

### Technical Attacks
Malware, credential theft, device compromise, network interception, cloud account compromise, exploitation of software vulnerabilities.

### Social Engineering
Phishing, impersonation, recruitment attempts, fraudulent interviews, trust exploitation, blackmail.

### Physical Attacks
Device theft, office intrusion, border searches, hidden surveillance, equipment tampering.

### Information Collection
Open-source intelligence, metadata analysis, contact mapping, public records, doxxing.

### Supply Chain Attacks
Compromised software updates, malicious dependencies, third-party service compromise, insider compromise.

---

## 8. Phase Five — Evaluate Operational Consequences

Risk assessment extends beyond confidentiality. Potential consequences include:

- Exposure of confidential sources
- Loss of anonymity
- Financial damage
- Reputational damage
- Operational disruption
- Physical harm
- Arrest or detention
- Exposure of family members
- Loss of public trust

The objective is to evaluate the real-world impact of compromise — not merely technical severity.

---

## 9. Phase Six — Implement Layered Operational Security

No single defensive measure is sufficient. SCOPE promotes defense in depth through multiple complementary layers.

### Technical Controls
Full-disk encryption, strong unique passwords via password manager, multi-factor authentication, timely software updates, secure backups.

### Operational Controls
Identity verification before sharing sensitive information, need-to-know information sharing, separation of sensitive and routine activities, travel security planning, incident response procedures.

### Physical Controls
Secure device storage, screen privacy in public environments, awareness during sensitive meetings, protection against theft or seizure.

### Human Controls
Security awareness training, trusted communication procedures, vetting of new contacts, emergency contact protocols.

Layered controls reduce the likelihood that the failure of any single safeguard results in systemic compromise.

---

## 10. Guiding Principles

- Protect people before technology
- Treat confidential sources as the highest-value assets
- Consider operational context alongside technical risk
- Assume attacks may combine digital, physical, legal, and social methods
- Reassess threats continuously as circumstances change
- Prioritize proportional, practical controls that users can sustain over time

---

## 11. Conclusion

The SCOPE Framework expands threat modeling beyond software systems and enterprise infrastructure by recognizing that modern attacks increasingly target people and the ecosystems surrounding them.

For journalists, activists, and other high-risk individuals, security is not achieved solely through encryption or hardened devices. It depends on understanding how people, communications, identities, documents, physical environments, and operational routines interact — and how an adversary may exploit those interactions.

By systematically identifying protected assets, assessing adversary capabilities, analyzing attack vectors, evaluating consequences, and implementing layered operational security, SCOPE provides a structured methodology for reducing risk while preserving the ability to work effectively in challenging environments.

> The goal is not merely to protect devices or data — but to protect the human network that makes critical work possible.
